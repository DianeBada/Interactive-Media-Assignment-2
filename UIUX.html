Elements I made use of in my Website

Von Restorff Effect
My Nav Bar includes various buttons that navigate clients to various pages, going from the homepage, blog page, style guide, and so on. They all appear to be identical, with the exception of the Portfolio page, as here I would like for clients' thoughtfulness regarding that button. This is where I would like them to survey my past works and potentially call me for future work. As indicated by the Von Restorff Effect, "when multiple similar objects are present, one that differs from the rest is most likely remembered" (Goldberg, 2022). This design was purposeful as I observed this guideline, to attempt to assist clients with recalling that my Portfolio page is unique in relation to different pages and that the page means quite a bit to see. I figure one thing that could be an issue with it, is that it is at the left end of the page. I will play around with the existing design of my nav-bar, to check whether I could find a preferred course of action over the one I have now.

CTA – Call to Action
On my homepage, I have a concise meaning of what's going on with the whole website, and I have a button there that is named "Explore" that prompts the client to click on it and investigate the sum of the page. This is the piece of content, known call to action, that "encourages the audience to do something" (Goldberg, 2022). Later on, I might want to utilize more CTAs, particularly in my portfolio segment, as this would help in the route of the space inside the site. It would provide clients guidance and wouldn't permit them to have a lost or confused outlook on what to do. I think this was what I confronted particularly in my portfolio area, as clients were not exactly certain with respect to what they should do and the elements of specific parts there.

Alignment
“Good alignment is invisible, i.e., this doesn’t have to be a literal line in your design” (Goldberg, 2022). I think another concept that I struggled with, was good alignment, especially within my blog section. Good alignment was hard for me to accomplish, only due to the fact that I thought my blog entries were placed in a weird unflattering position. It did not look pleasing to the eyes and hence, I had to rearrange it and find some sort of balance between all elements placed on the screen.

Functionals vs Aesthetically pleasing?
“‘Should I design it to be functional,’ the students say, ‘or to be aesthetically pleasing?’ This is the most often heard, the most understandable, and yet the most mixed-up question in design today. ‘Do you want it to look good or to work?’ Barricades are erected between what are really just two of the many aspects of function.” (Papanek, 1984) After reading through this reading, I had to really think about whether I wanted my website to be functional or “pretty”. Why couldn’t I have both? I saw that it was easier to make the website aesthetically pleasing than to have it fully functional. I also think that’s only because of proficiency in regard to actually coding with Html, JavaScript, and CSS. I feel that the functionality of the website will only improve from here onwards as my skills in making websites will increase over time. So, for the current moment, my website, I feel, is leaning more towards the aesthetic side. Also, I realized that this was my way of covering up things I’m lacking in terms of the functionality of the website.
